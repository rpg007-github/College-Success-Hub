How to Use These Resources

1

Choose what fits where you are right now

If you're struggling with basic calculations, start with Math Fluency. If you feel overwhelmed by coursework, try the Study Skills guides. If you're not sure what you want to study, explore the Finding Direction resources. There's no wrong place to begin.

2

Work through content at your own pace

Every guide has clear explanations followed by practice opportunities. Take your time. Review sections that feel challenging. The interactive tools are designed to help you learn, not test what you don't know yet.

3

Focus on progress, not perfection

Building confidence is about consistent small improvements. Even working through one section or trying a few practice problems is meaningful progress. Come back when you're ready for more. Every return visit counts.

4

Everyone learns differently and at different speeds

What matters is finding approaches that work for you. These resources offer multiple ways to engage with material — explanations, examples, flashcards, and practice quizzes. Use what helps, skip what doesn't, and trust that you're building the foundation for success.

Questions Worth Sitting With

1

What problems do you find genuinely interesting?

Not "what are you good at" — that comes later. Start with what kinds of questions you find yourself thinking about without being asked to. Health and the body? How systems and economies work? Language and storytelling? Environmental problems? Technology and how it changes behavior?

2

What kind of work environment fits you?

Do you want to work with people directly, or mostly with data, ideas, or objects? Indoors or out? In an office, a lab, a hospital, a school, a studio? These aren't trivial preferences — they're real constraints that should shape your direction.

3

What have you tried that felt right, even a little?

Part-time jobs, volunteer work, classes you actually enjoyed, extracurriculars — these are real data about what might fit. The goal is to collect more data, not to already know the answer.

It's Okay Not to Know

Many students feel pressure to have their entire life mapped out by sophomore year. The truth is, most successful people changed direction multiple times. Exploration and uncertainty are normal — and healthy — parts of finding your path. The students who seem certain often aren't.
